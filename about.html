Funny Paper Jumping Jacks

A vintage collection devoted exclusively to the Imagerie de Wissembourg

This website presents a private collection of more than 150 original articulated paper lithographs produced in Wissembourg / Weißenburg. Collected by Ralf Geissler, it focuses only on prints from the Wissembourg printworks and its successors. The aim is simple: to show one printing tradition clearly, not to mix it with others.

The Wissembourg Printworks

The Imagerie de Wissembourg was founded by Jean-Frédéric Wentzel in 1835. Later the business continued under Frédéric-Charles Wentzel, Camille Burckardt, Hermann Junck, Emile Schenck, René Ackermann and Charles Ackermann. Over time the printworks produced religious sheets, views, decorative prints, cut-out models and articulated figures such as pantins and jumping jacks.

Why This Collection Is So Focused

This collection is limited to Wissembourg material by design. That focus helps reveal patterns. Motifs return. Formats repeat. Publisher names change. Variants become easier to compare. The jumping jacks were playful objects, but they were also part of a larger commercial world with its own style and habits.

Vintage, Nostalgia and the Physical Object

These prints are vintage objects. They carry nostalgia, but they are more than nostalgic. They are offline, tactile and haptic. They were made to be cut out, assembled, handled and kept. In a life that becomes more digital every year, good old physical things like these gain value again. That is part of their appeal today.

Research Background

The Wissembourg printworks has a real exhibition and research history. In 1999, the exhibition Heilige, Herrscher, Hampelmänner / Saints, souverains, pantins was shown at the Badisches Landesmuseum Karlsruhe and at the Grange aux Dîmes in Wissembourg. The catalogue remains an important reference. Later work, including the Strasbourg publication Des mondes de papier: L’imagerie populaire de Wissembourg, confirmed the scale and importance of Wissembourg production.

Wissembourg Today

Recent institutional work in Wissembourg has shown how large this printed world once was. In 2022 the city acquired the historic fonds of the former business. After sorting work completed in 2023, more than 460,000 surviving sheets yielded a little over 5,000 distinct models. Even a specialized group such as articulated figures belongs to that wider archive.

About This Website

This site is meant as a focused reference for Wissembourg articulated prints in private hands. Over time it will grow from a thematic overview into a more precise record with individual entries, consistent descriptions and clearer publication data.

About the Collector

Ralf Geissler assembled this collection out of a longstanding interest in overlooked print culture, historical graphics and the borderland between toy, image and document. The aim is not just to preserve rare survivals, but to present them as evidence of one specific printmaking tradition: Wissembourg, and Wissembourg only.

Looking Ahead

In the longer term, this website is also intended to grow into a small web shop for nostalgic prints and posters inspired by this world of vintage paper imagery.
