Funny Paper Jumping Jacks

A vintage collection devoted exclusively to the Imagerie de Wissembourg

Highlights from the Collection

This website presents highlights from a collection of more than 150 original articulated paper lithographs from the Imagerie de Wissembourg in Alsace, France, dating from roughly 1880 to 1920.

Cut It Out and Have Fun

These prints were made to be pasted onto cardboard or thin plywood, cut out and assembled with string into moving jumping jacks. When the string was pulled, the figures came to life. Some simply moved their arms and legs. Others performed small actions: a shoe shiner polished shoes, a violinist moved his bow, a ballerina danced. They were playful objects, but also highly visual ones, designed to surprise, amuse and attract attention.

Hang It Up and Your Guests Will Be Amazed

Today, these figures can still be enjoyed in different ways. They may be framed as colourful and unusual wall pieces, but they can also serve as conversation pieces in the old sense: objects that invite questions, stories and closer attention. Their charm lies not only in their imagery, but also in their rarity. Very few people have such prints in their homes. They are decorative, nostalgic and slightly eccentric, and that is part of what makes them so appealing.

Why Wissembourg

This collection is devoted to Wissembourg for personal as well as historical reasons. Many years ago, I bought my first paper jumping jack in an antiquarian bookshop in Munich. It was this Wissembourg figure. I had it framed, and that was the beginning. From then on, I became increasingly fascinated by the jumping jacks from Weißenburg and gradually expanded the collection in that direction. I could have collected figures from Épinal or Neuruppin as well, but Wissembourg came first, and that first encounter shaped the collection.

Alsace and a Borderland History

There is also a deeper reason. I have long loved Alsace and France more generally. The history of Alsace is especially compelling because it moved back and forth between German and French rule. That tension is also reflected in the output of the Wissembourg printworks. Its motifs belong to a region with a layered and unsettled identity. In that sense, these figures are not only decorative objects. They are also witnesses to a borderland and to a culture shaped by political change.

What the Figures Reveal About Their Time

These prints also reveal a great deal about the world that produced them. They show poverty, social outsiders and a taste for popular entertainment. Harlequins, performers, musicians, dancers and comic figures point to a culture shaped by theatre, cabaret and public amusement. At the same time, the prints are full of social stereotypes and fixed “types”. Some of these now read very clearly as racist clichés, for example in the depiction of Chinese, African or Indigenous figures. Others reflect harsh attitudes towards class, family life and discipline, including scenes of violence in child-rearing. Military figures are everywhere, which says much about the place of soldiers and martial values in that period. Yet the prints also show how important performance, spectacle and the arts were in everyday popular culture.

The Wissembourg Printworks in Context

The history of the Wissembourg printworks deserves attention in its own right. Alongside Épinal and Neuruppin, Wissembourg was one of the major centres of popular print production in Central Europe. Founded by Jean-Frédéric Wentzel in 1835, the business developed into a major producer of popular prints and continued under several successors well into the twentieth century. Its output was broad and commercial, but also remarkably inventive. It ranged from religious sheets and educational prints to decorative images, cut-out models and articulated figures. The jumping jacks belong to this larger history of production, but they are only one part of it.

Research, Museums and Wider Context

That broader history has also been explored in museums and research. In 1999, the exhibition Heilige, Herrscher, Hampelmänner / Saints, souverains, pantins was shown both at the Badisches Landesmuseum Karlsruhe and at the Grange aux Dîmes in Wissembourg. The accompanying catalogue remains an important point of reference. In Wissembourg today, prints from the historic output of the printworks are preserved and presented in a much broader museum context. The articulated figures appear there as one element within a far larger body of work. This website takes a different approach. Here, the paper jumping jacks stand at the centre.

Looking Ahead

The goal is to expand this website during 2026 into a webshop for nostalgic prints inspired by the original Wissembourg figures. The shop will offer reproductions only, not original works. Planned formats include posters and framed reproductions. The idea is not only to document this remarkable body of material, but also to bring some of its charm, colour and character into homes today.
