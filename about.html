Funny Paper Jumping Jacks

A vintage collection devoted exclusively to the Imagerie de Wissembourg

This website is about one printmaking tradition only: Wissembourg. The collection brings together more than 150 original articulated paper lithographs from the Imagerie de Wissembourg and its successors. It is not a mixed overview of popular prints from many places. Its focus is narrow on purpose.

One Printing House, Many Images

Jean-Frédéric Wentzel founded the Wissembourg printworks in 1835. The business later continued under Frédéric-Charles Wentzel, Camille Burckardt, Hermann Junck, Emile Schenck, René Ackermann and Charles Ackermann. Across roughly a century, the printworks produced an enormous range of images for a mass public. These included devotional sheets, city views, scenes of everyday life, military subjects, educational prints, cut-out models and articulated figures such as pantins and jumping jacks.

Why Wissembourg Only?

The narrow focus makes the material easier to read. Once different print houses are mixed together, too much gets blurred. Wissembourg has its own subjects, formats, colouring habits and publisher marks. The articulated figures belong to that larger visual world. They were not odd exceptions. They were one product line inside a much broader image business.

A European Image Factory

Wissembourg was not a small local curiosity. Research on the firm has shown how widely its prints circulated. The business sold far beyond Alsace and built a European reach. That scale matters for this collection. Even the most playful jumping jack belongs to a larger commercial and cultural network of production, sale and use.

Articulated Figures and Paper Play

The collection concentrates on movable figures: cut, jointed and assembled prints made for play, display and surprise. These objects sit at the border between toy, print and design. They are light, fragile and often damaged by use. That is exactly why surviving examples matter. They preserve not only their printed image, but also traces of cutting, pinning, repair and handling.

Research, Museums and the Book Behind This Project

The Wissembourg printworks has already been the subject of serious museum and research work. In 1999, the exhibition Heilige, Herrscher, Hampelmänner / Saints, souverains, pantins was shown in Karlsruhe and Wissembourg. The catalogue that grew out of that exhibition remains a key reference for the history of the printworks and for the place of articulated figures within its production. Later publications, including Des mondes de papier: L’imagerie populaire de Wissembourg, deepened that research and confirmed the historical importance of Wissembourg imagery.

The Surviving Wissembourg Archive

Recent work in Wissembourg has made the scale of the surviving material even clearer. In 2022, the city acquired the historic fonds of the former business. After a major sorting campaign completed in 2023, more than 460,000 surviving sheets yielded a little over 5,000 distinct models. The material includes religious, military, decorative and ludo-educational prints, including pantins and other cut-out objects. This website shows only one small corner of that much larger world.

Vintage, Nostalgia and the Physical Object

These prints are vintage objects. They carry nostalgia, but they are more than nostalgic. They are offline, tactile and haptic. They were made to be cut out, touched, moved and kept. In a life that becomes more digital every year, good old physical things like these gain value again. Their appeal is visual, but also material. They invite the eye and the hand.

About This Website

This site is meant as a focused working reference for Wissembourg articulated prints in private hands. Over time it will grow from a visual overview into a more precise record with individual object pages, clearer descriptions, better dating and more consistent publication details.

About the Collector

Ralf Geissler assembled this collection out of a longstanding interest in overlooked print culture, historical graphics and the strange borderland between toy, image and document. The aim is not only to preserve rare survivals, but to make them legible as part of one specific printmaking tradition: Wissembourg, and Wissembourg only.

Looking Ahead

In the longer term, this website is also intended to grow into a web shop for nostalgic prints and posters inspired by this world of vintage paper imagery.
